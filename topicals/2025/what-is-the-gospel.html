What Is the Gospel?

Distant City | Faith, Truth, and Response

Two men stared into my soul as an 18-year-old in a new place. I had grown up around Christians, served in my church, and would have called myself a believer. Yet, when they asked me a simple yet profound question—“What is the Gospel?”—I realized I had no real answer.

I joked, “Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John!” Then, after thinking for a moment (a rare feat for a teenager), I said, “Jesus died for our sins so we can have eternal life.”

But something about that response felt hollow. Maybe it was how I said it, or maybe I didn’t say enough. They handed me a book titled What Is the Gospel?

That book didn’t just give me an answer—it gave me a question: Am I saved? If so, how do I know?

For the next year, I searched for clarity.

The Bridge Illustration

One of the clearest explanations I found was the Bridge Illustration—from St. Catherine of Siena. Humanity and God are separated by a vast chasm of sin. “For the wages of sin is death” (Romans 6:23), an eternal separation from God.

But the verse doesn’t end there: “...but the free gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.” The cross becomes the bridge across the chasm, allowing us to be united with God. This is the Gospel in its simplest form.

There’s a heavy emphasis on the gift—salvation cannot be earned. And yet, something still felt incomplete.

A Simple Framework: The Five R’s

1. Relationship

We were created for union with God. In the Garden of Eden, humanity walked with God in perfect harmony. This was our intended state—relationship.

2. Rebellion

But then, the Fall. Adam and Eve’s sin introduced separation. This isn’t just their story—it’s ours. “All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God” (Romans 3:23).

3. Reconciliation

God, in His love, sent His Son to take on flesh, suffer, and die in our place. The cross is not just an image of suffering—it’s an invitation back to relationship.

4. Recreation

The Gospel doesn’t stop at a transaction—it transforms. “If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation” (2 Corinthians 5:17). The Christian life is one of sanctification.

5. Response

Jesus calls, “Follow me.” This isn’t passive belief—it’s active discipleship. “Go therefore and make disciples of all nations” (Matthew 28:19).

Salvation: Gift or Task?

Romans 6 tells us eternal life is a gift. James 2 tells us faith without works is dead. The tension is resolved in Ephesians 2:8–10.

We are saved by grace—but that grace produces transformation. Salvation is both a gift and a lifelong journey of conversion.

The Parable of the Seeds

The seed is the same—the difference is the soil. Hearing the Gospel is not enough. Even believing is not enough if it never takes root.

The question is not merely, “Do you believe?” but “Has it transformed you?”

Who Sits on the Throne?

If Christ is truly Lord, He must sit on the throne of our hearts. Faith isn’t adding Jesus—it’s surrendering to Him.

“If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross daily and follow me.” (Luke 9:23)

The Gospel Demands a Response

The invitation is open. The question remains: What will you do with it?
