The Technocratic Problem

Distant City | Sobriety, Freedom, and the Custody of the Soul

He flipped open his phone, dialed a number, and raised the silver machine to his ear. You could hear the ringing. You could see the smile on his face. What shocked me was not the device, but what it represented.

This was 2023, and I had just met a man who used a flip phone for everything.

But there was more. He was a missionary.

He fundraised his salary on it. He called students on it. He called his wife on it. He listened to music on it. He used it for photos and GPS. I thought he was crazy. I’m sure you do too.

And yet, this trend is growing.

Among college students. Gen Z. Even some millennials. Across the U.S., people are picking up Light Phones, dumb phones, flip phones, even BlackBerrys. I recently saw that Charlie Puth tweeted he thinks 2026 will be the year we all go analog and leave the digital world behind.

Imagine it. The first year it would feel strange to not be on your phone all day. No doomscrolling. No compulsive email checks. We would go from checking our phones 176 times a day and spending over eight hours on screens to something that looks suspiciously like freedom.

We are hungry for freedom because we are in slavery.

A slavery of the mind, the will, and the passions.

Groups like Fight the New Drug have done extensive research showing that porn can affect the brain in ways similar to heroin. Aza Raskin, the designer of the infinite scroll feature, has publicly stated that he regrets creating it after seeing how many minds have been captivated by social media slop.

And yet I wonder: do you remember choosing this life?

Was this a free yes?
Did you give real consent?

Can an eight-year-old on a new account on X meaningfully consent to this?

Sobriety, outside of alcohol, means being serious, sensible, and deliberate. Is it sensible for every twelve-year-old boy and girl to carry a device capable of accessing nearly anything on the internet?

I don’t raise this as fear-mongering, but as a sober observation of how senseless our digital life has become.

When was the last time you read the terms and conditions of an iPhone update? When did you allow it to track your location? When did you allow it to sell your data to companies that want to profit from your habits, desires, and attention?

Have you ever read the terms and conditions of your phone?

Here is the point: we no longer have custody of our intellect, our will, or our passions.

Our desires are stimulated before we ever choose them. We buy speakers we didn’t need. We scroll without silence. We anesthetize instead of discern.

The question is not whether a thing is good, but whether we are choosing it freely.

One in ten people in the United States has Alcohol Use Disorder. At the same time, health authorities now warn that no level of alcohol consumption is safe — and yet we continue.

This is the technocratic problem.

We search until we find permission. Confirmation bias baptizes desire. Groupthink replaces freedom. Statistics replace judgment.

A rightly ordered person receives data, judges it with reason, and governs the passions that arise. A wrongly ordered person reacts, repairs, and repeats.

One thrives. The other survives.

The enemy does not need us to deny God. He only needs us distracted.

Distracted by noise. Consumption. Bread and circuses. Busy enough to forget God.

If we do not look for God in the world, we will simply find the world.

The task is not retreat, but resistance. Replace slavery with freedom. Replace noise with stillness. Reclaim custody of the soul.

There is a world hungry for truth.

Who will tell them?
